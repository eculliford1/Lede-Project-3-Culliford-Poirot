Elizabeth Culliford Project 3 Poirot

The Case of the Missing Project

By Elizabeth Culliford





I wanted to analyse plot summaries to learn more about the likely formulas in novels by the prolific British detective writer Agatha Christie. To narrow the project down, I focused on the 33 published, full-length novels where the detective was the famous Belgian sleuth Hercule Poirot.

A key aim for this project was to use real-world visualisation techniques. I wanted to use the aesthetics of some kind of family game like Bingo or cards to give a sense that there were a fixed number of suspects or options, and suggest the idea of common characteristics among the novels.

(It wasn't realistic in my timeframe to get into all of these hallmarks but just as a taster, there is usually a country home, an inheritance fight or extramarital affairs, some poison, false identities and many, many items left lying at the crime scene with the murderer's initials on them.)

I thought about using cards from the murder mystery board game Cluedo but then found a pack of Agatha Christie playing cards online, which I photographed and designed the page around. Another aim was to focus on the story of the project itself and weave the write-up of any technical skills, data decisions and visualisations into this page.

Whodunnit?


Male and female murderers

Finding:
I found that the murderer in Poirot novels was more often a man.

I think my data collection didn't give the richest picture of different killers' gender however because my 'M/F' choice for each murderer collapsed the main 'female murderer' type and the 'female accomplice,' who is often on the side in these novels helping their accomplice-lover, and the gender difference may have felt starker if I had teased those out.

It also would have been interesting to analyse which methods were preferred by female and male killers (poison vs. bludgeoning for example) but I grouped together the methods of multiple killers within one novel, making that too broad in my database.






Finding:The victims were more often women, though the differences weren't as stark as I expected. I could have separated out the data more finely to emphasise when the woman was the intended target of the murder or when she was collateral damage.

(Note: if you are in a Poirot novel and you know who the murderer is, keep quiet. There are a lot of deaths of observant also-rans who figure the killer out and have to be silenced.)





The categorisations weren't always simple - e.g. a number of people die by suicide and so there were victims/deaths within the novel not always attached to a murderer, and sometimes the murderer killed themselves. In other instances, it wasn't made clear in the plot if someone had died accidentally or of natural causes or whether they were actually murdered.

Some source sites also include old murders that had happened before the novel's timeframe began, or attempted murders and those methods, which I aimed to discount. There were also victims counted in some summaries because people have been wrongfully convicted for deaths and died through that process or were hanged for crimes. I decided to name any possible victims to keep that qualitative data recorded for readers to see and I ended up only using numerical counts of victims to find male and female ratios.



Stay away from...


The idea for this analysis was answering the cliche of 'the husband did it' or 'the butler did it' with actual data analysis from the Poirot novels, so I wanted to include more qualitative information here about the position/relation of the murderer to the victim. After analysing the plot of the first few novels, I decided motives were too difficult to get into for this project so these descriptions also are the best hint at those.

Finding: If we discount the 'avengers' (coming to them next) then the most likely killer was a tie between a family member (a lot of inheritance motives) or a criminal element from outside, such as a con artist, thief or spy.

(This surprised me as I think of Poirot murderers being more intimate connections than outside criminals. However, the current spouse was also a likely killer, and if I had grouped together 'current and former spouses/lovers' then that would have been a winning category. This made me realise that sometimes I can break out categories too finely when I know they will be major ones.)




This data analysis and the visualisation through the chart did ended up skewed by the killers in Murder on the Orient Express because there are 12 avengers of Daisy Armstrong's death and they all help to kill Cassetti. I thought about including them as one murderer because it was only one murder, but then this would have confused the logic by which I was including male and female murderers who had both killed a person. I also added Poirot into this category for his murder in Curtain: Poirot's Last Case but looking back there maybe should have been others acting in revenge who fit into this category.




The murder weapon


Finding: The most common method of killing was poison.This was a huge category that covered drug overdoses, poison in tea or candy, dentist chair murders and bedside glasses of water switched with acid. Cups were swapped around and swapped back. Traces were left and not left.

The next most common method was stabbing, with a knife or dagger. I decided to categorise through weapon rather than method so knife/dagger also encompassed a few slit throats. Being bludgeoned, or hit over the head, and also being shot by a gun were the next most common, with strangulation coming up fairly often too.



Some of the very specific details got lost in the broad categorization of weapons and methods - e.g. people being hit with a sugar hammer, drowned in an apple-bobbing bucket or killed by sandbags at a girls' boarding school.

Staging the perfect crime


I used a dark red and blue colour scheme for the charts, mostly to work with the colours of the playing cards set though there are also blood connotations which worked for the murder mystery theme. I also tested out using screen descriptions of my charts for the first time. I used a formatting style I had liked before and experimented with a new font family. I tried to keep consistent hierarchies through various elements (e.g. yellow highlighted spoiler alert, red chart colour schemes, headers with a moustache motif to indicate new sections, bold text for data findings and italics for my asides.)

I was pleased with incorporating the real-life visuals as well as web images like the moustache images to symbolise Poirot. I shot the playing cards close up against white backgrounds which I digitally removed, though I struggled breaking them out of uniform sizings that I'd set. I used the cards to emphasise the charts in more eye-catching ways and tie the piece together visually.

I also tried to visually show more behind-the-scenes art to go with my emphasis on weaving the write-up and my thoughts into the project findings (e.g. photos of me adjusting the cards or my rough sketches of the board game inspirations I could use.)


Red herrings and false starts


I tried to find an existing dataset I could analyse and got in touch with some Agatha Christie fans who had written on their websites about their findings from databases they had put together, but I didn't hear back. I decided to create my own database using online synopses from the site everythingagatha.com, Wikipedia entries for the novels and other plot summaries.

I entered the data manually using the categories of book title, publication date, victim/s names, victim M/F, murderer/s names, murderer M/F, character description/position of the murderer (focusing on their relation to the victim), the method/weapon used and the broad primary setting for the novel.

This shifted the focus of Project 3 onto data collection - which I haven't had as a main focus in a project before - and decisions around categorization, meaning the data analysis became a smaller manual part of the project. I tried to think backwards from the types of questions I wanted to answer and categorise as cleanly as I could around those. I realized I was changing my mind about certain categorisations as I went and wished I had kept more notes in that data collection stage, as I had learned to do during the analysis stage.

I decided to make a searchable table to show the working data that I was making my charts from, so readers could also draw their own conclusions or challenge the ways I had categorised data-points. I also wanted to make this available because I had struggled by not being able to see the databases used by others writing or making charts about these books' plots.
